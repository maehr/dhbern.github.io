Conceptual and Ethical Framework

Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten (Version 2) is an interdisciplinary research initiative examining how metadata practices in digital archives and datasets can inadvertently reproduce discrimination. At its core, the project interrogates the notion that metadata – the descriptive information about historical sources, artifacts, or research data – is a neutral technical layer. Instead, it recognizes that metadata schemas and vocabularies often embed the values and biases of their creators and institutional contexts. Classification systems and descriptive standards come with normative assumptions that may privilege certain groups or worldviews while marginalizing others. The project’s goal is to critically reflect on these normative classifications and uncover biases in metadata schemas, controlled vocabularies, and archival cataloging practices, in order to develop more inclusive approaches. By questioning how we label and organize information, Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten seeks to ensure that the act of describing digital cultural heritage and research data does not perpetuate historical prejudices or structural inequalities.

This conceptual and ethical framing means addressing a range of biases that can be hidden in metadata. For example, widely used library and archival vocabularies have historically enforced gender norms: the German Integrated Authority File (GND), a common standard, long provided only masculine forms of terms and recognized only a binary gender category (plus an “unknown/other” option), reflecting implicit sexism and cis-normativity (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten am Beispiel von Stadt.Geschichte.Basel). Likewise, many archival catalogs inherit colonial biases – descriptions and subject headings created in imperial contexts that use derogatory or Eurocentric terminology. Digitized collections of colonial-era photographs, for instance, often retain minimal or one-sided descriptions provided by the colonial institutions, lacking critical context and using outdated language (DHQ: Digital Humanities Quarterly: Sensitivity and Access: Unlocking the Colonial Visual Archive with Machine Learning). Such cases illustrate how structural discrimination can persist in metadata: seemingly innocuous catalog entries may carry forward racism, sexism, ableism, or other forms of bias. Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten addresses these issues by scrutinizing how metadata is produced and used. It advocates for practices like using gender-inclusive vocabularies (e.g. adopting the GenderOpen thesaurus instead of exclusively male or binary terms (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten am Beispiel von Stadt.Geschichte.Basel)) and providing historical context for sensitive terms or content. In doing so, the project aligns with broader currents in critical data studies that argue for an awareness of power dynamics in all information infrastructures. Ultimately, this framework situates metadata creation as not just a technical task, but an ethical responsibility to represent knowledge in a fair and non-discriminatory manner.

Background and Initial Case Study

The project originated in the context of a digital history initiative and has evolved from there. Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten was first developed as part of the Stadt.Geschichte.Basel project – a public history portal exploring the urban history of Basel. In its initial version, the team of researchers identified the need for guidelines to ensure that the metadata describing historical sources in that portal was inclusive and did not inadvertently echo past prejudices. This led to the creation of a comprehensive handbook (Version 1) on how to create “discrimination-free” metadata, using the Basel project’s data as a case study. Published in 2024, the handbook (in German) serves as a practical guide to producing non-discriminatory metadata for historical sources and research datasets (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten). Aimed at professional historians, archivists, librarians, and others in the cultural heritage and research data communities, it walks readers through the process of designing metadata that adheres to open data and FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) while critically addressing problematic terms or classifications (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten). The authors, Moritz Mähr and Noëlle Schnegg, structured the handbook with step-by-step instructions and illustrated case examples drawn from the Basel archives, demonstrating how machine-readable metadata can enrich research and teaching and even shape the interpretation of historical sources (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten). Crucially, the handbook was conceived as a publicly accessible “living document,” intended to evolve with community feedback and new insights. It explicitly committed to an inclusive, non-discriminatory representation of historical content in metadata practices (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten). This first phase of the project established a foundation of concepts, use cases, and initial best practices, rooted firmly in an academic understanding of both metadata standards and the ethical considerations of historical scholarship.

Version 2: Broadening Scope and Goals

In Version 2, Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten is being restructured and expanded beyond its original case-study focus. The new phase of the project transitions from a localized example (Basel’s history collections) to a broader platform supporting a wide range of research-driven digital projects. This means that while the insights from the Stadt.Geschichte.Basel experience remain valuable, Version 2 generalizes and adapts them to benefit diverse contexts – from other city or regional history portals, to museum and library databases, to academic research data repositories in the humanities and social sciences. The aim is to make the project’s guidance more universally applicable across different types of collections and metadata standards, without losing the nuanced, scholarly perspective that shaped the original handbook. In practical terms, Version 2 will continue to be grounded in rigorous research, but will offer more flexible and wide-ranging examples that users can draw upon in their own projects. It also places a stronger emphasis on being a resource that is up-to-date with ongoing discussions in the field, essentially bridging theory and practice for multiple communities.

Key elements of Version 2 include:

  • Broader Scope: A shift from a single case study to addressing common metadata challenges across various digital collections and datasets. Version 2 is designed to support historians and information professionals working not just on Basel’s history, but on any project where descriptive standards risk reinforcing bias. It will draw on examples from different domains (e.g. ethnographic archives, library catalogs, oral history databases) to illustrate its recommendations.
  • Practical Toolkit: An expanded set of guidelines and tools for implementation. The project will provide updated checklists, templates, and best-practice examples for creating inclusive metadata. These practical resources will show how to handle problematic terminology, how to choose or customize controlled vocabularies, and how to document context for sensitive materials. By including multiple use cases, Version 2 ensures that the advice can be tailored to different institutional settings and metadata schemas (such as Dublin Core, museum cataloging standards, or research data ontologies).
  • Academic Integration: Ongoing engagement with scholarly research to inform metadata practices. The project stays attuned to the latest findings in fields like digital humanities, archival science, library and information science, and critical race/gender studies. This ensures that its recommendations are not only technically sound but also in dialogue with contemporary academic debates about ethics and representation in data. For example, Version 2 considers frameworks like the CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance and the OpenGLAM principles for open cultural heritage data, integrating their emphasis on community agency and ethical access into metadata guidance.
  • Community and Continuity: Maintaining the “living document” philosophy. Version 2 will invite a broader community of contributors and users to discuss and refine its content, for instance via an open platform or repository. By crowdsourcing expertise and feedback from archivists, curators, researchers, and the public, the project can continuously update its recommendations. This collaborative approach not only keeps the guidance current but also embodies the project’s ethos of inclusivity – recognizing that building discrimination-free metadata is an ongoing, collective effort.

Throughout these developments, Version 2 remains committed to its academic rigor combined with practical utility. The restructuring is carefully balanced so that the project’s output is useful to on-the-ground practitioners (providing clear instructions and examples) while also being deeply informed by theory (engaging with questions of how knowledge organization can enforce or challenge social norms). In essence, the broadened Version 2 aims to function as both a scholarly resource and a hands-on manual for enacting ethical metadata standards across a variety of digital initiatives.

Interdisciplinary Team and Approach

An essential strength of Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten is its interdisciplinary nature, which is carried forward and enhanced in Version 2. The project is a collaboration between researchers with backgrounds in history, digital humanities, and data ethics. Levyn Bürki, Moritz Mähr, and Noëlle Schnegg together contribute a wide spectrum of expertise that shapes the project’s approach. This interdisciplinary team ensures that multiple perspectives are interwoven: the historical viewpoint brings awareness of context, narrative, and the often sensitive content of archival materials; the digital humanities and information science viewpoint contributes knowledge of metadata standards, data modeling, and platform design; and the critical data studies perspective offers tools to interrogate power, bias, and ideology within information systems. By working at the intersection of these fields, the team is able to critically analyze existing metadata schemas and practices (asking, for instance, how categories were formed and whose viewpoints they reflect) and at the same time propose realistic, implementable solutions for improvement (such as new documentation methods or the adoption of alternative vocabularies).

The contributions of Bürki, Mähr, and Schnegg are evident in the project’s blending of theory and practice. Mähr and Schnegg’s work on the initial handbook leveraged their historical training and practical experience with a real-world dataset, ensuring that the guidelines were grounded in actual archival content and user needs (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten). With the addition of Levyn Bürki in Version 2, the project gains further depth in digital scholarship and data-centric methodologies, reinforcing its ability to bridge technical implementation with cultural critique. The interdisciplinary collaboration is not only reflected in the content (which draws examples from historical case studies and addresses technical standards in the same breath) but also in the project’s methodology. The team engages with literature ranging from archival theory to semantic web design, and it consults with practitioners in libraries, archives, and museums to validate its approaches. This collegial, cross-disciplinary modus operandi is crucial for tackling a problem as complex as metadata-based discrimination: it takes into account that bias in metadata is at once a social/cultural issue and a technical/design issue, and thus must be approached from multiple angles.

Towards Inclusive Metadata Practice

Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten (Version 2) represents a forward-looking effort to foster equitable and reflective metadata practices in the management of digital cultural heritage and research data. By remaining grounded in scholarly analysis while providing concrete guidance, the project serves as a bridge between abstract ethical principles and day-to-day metadata work. It prompts academics and collection managers alike to pause and consider how the categories and labels they apply might be reinforcing outdated narratives or exclusionary norms, and it equips them with strategies to counteract those tendencies. Whether it is by recommending the use of gender-neutral language, encouraging the addition of historical context to descriptions of sensitive materials, or suggesting the revision of classification schemes that otherize certain groups, the project’s recommendations help practitioners make tangible changes toward inclusivity.

The impact of this project is both immediate and long-term. In the immediate sense, Version 2 will offer an accessible online resource (and associated documentation) that scholars, archivists, and digital humanists can consult when designing their databases or digital collections. Over the longer term, as the ideas disseminate and are adopted, it aspires to influence institutional policies and standards – contributing to updates in how metadata schemas are defined and how vocabularies are maintained. The broader vision is that metadata, often an unseen backbone of digital knowledge infrastructure, becomes consciously designed to be discrimination-free. This means future researchers and the public can discover and use digital collections without encountering the subtle biases or offensive terminologies that once might have gone unquestioned. By cleaning the “microdata” of history – the titles, tags, categories, and descriptions – the project helps pave the way for more just and accurate macro-narratives in historical interpretation and cultural memory.

In summary, Diskriminierungsfreie Metadaten (Version 2) stands as a crucial academic and practical endeavor to rethink how we describe our world. It underscores that the ethics of data are as important as the data itself. Through critical inquiry into normative classifications and active development of better practices, the project contributes to a more inclusive digital heritage landscape. It invites continued dialogue and collaboration, positioning itself as a living, evolving resource that will grow with the community it serves. By challenging biases embedded in metadata and championing inclusive alternatives, Version 2 of this project will support a new standard of metadata work – one that is aware, responsible, and free from discrimination, in service of both historical truth and social equity (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten am Beispiel von Stadt.Geschichte.Basel) (Handbuch zur Erstellung diskriminierungsfreier Metadaten für historische Quellen und Forschungsdaten).
